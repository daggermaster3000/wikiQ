During April 2021, frost destroyed more dans 85% of the apricot crops in Switzerland. The harvest, in the swiss-romande region was the worst in the past thirty years. This problematic is not an isolated event, frost damages tend to increase over the years and concerns many countries.

This year, the production of apricot in Switzerland incurred 85% of loss.

The costs of these damages were estimated over 5 millions of dollars.

Our spray will contain agents allowing us to combat a plant pathogen that accelerates freezing. This pathogene is a bacteria called P. Siringae and it's producing an ice nucleation protein. Our goal is then to fight agains this pathogene and simultaneously using anti-freeze protein produced in our lab.

Our three different approach will be combined in one product.

Bacteriophages are natural predators of bacteria. Their mode of action is quite similar to our own (eucaryotic) viral pathogens. Once the phage attacks the bacteria, in this case P. syringae pv. syringae (Pss), it inserts its genetic material into it. Our purpose is to exploit this mechanism to make it deliver into Pss a CRISPR/Cas9 construct, which will eventually be expressed and cut the gene responsible for the ice nucleation protein production, by aiming to disarm the plant pathogen. Learn more about this part of the project following this link.

Tailocins are proteins secreted by certain bacteria strains to attack others. These proteins are very similar in structure to phages except for the capsule. Tailocins are very specific and only attack one species. Our aim is to produce those protein in the lab and then add it to our product. Learn more about tailocins and their production following this link.

Anti-freeze proteins (AFP) are proteins produced by various organisms, from bacteria to plants or insects, to defend themselves from the cold. This protein has the particularity to bind forming ice and prevent its growing. The global mechanism is always the same, but each AFP has its own properties. We selected three of them to fight efficiently the frost. Click here to learn more about this part of the project.
