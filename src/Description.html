Description

Context

Plant frost damage is not only a physical phenomenon

Some bacteria species which live on plant surfaces were shown to promote ice crystal formation and thus to increase frost damage on plants. Contrary to popular belief, water does not only need negative temperatures to freeze. Ice formation also requires a certain template to organise the water molecules in their crystalline pattern. This template is called an ice nucleating center. The ice will then form a lattice on which free water molecules will attach, making the crystal grow. Minerals and small particles act as ice nucleating centers. Moreover, plant tissues themselves are also relatively bad ice nuclei: when frost sensitive plants are grown in sterile conditions, most of them can resist to temperature as low as – 5 °C to – 10 °C without frost damage. However, in the environment, frost sensitive plants suffer at temperature only slightly below 0 °C from frost damage which are caused by bacteria colonizing their surface, especially the species Pseudomonas syringae. Pseudomonas syringae produces an ice nucleation protein, called InaZ2, on its membrane which catalyses the formation of ice crystals and thus causes frost damage on the plant at higher temperature. The damage helps the bacteria to get in the plant as Pseudomonas syringae is also a plant pathogen. For these reasons, Pseudomonas syringae is a big problem for apricot producers.3 Our project therefore aims at providing two approaches to fight these bacteria.
